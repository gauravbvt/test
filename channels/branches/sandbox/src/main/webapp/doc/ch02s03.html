Building a Plan

A Plan is built by information sharing planners (Planners) who carry out Activities defined in the ISP Method in order to move the Plan toward completion.

Activities

Activities either alter the scope of a Plan, bring the Plan closer to completion, or improve the quality and value of the Procedures generated from it.

Activities are organized into these four groups:

  • Scoping (a Plan),

  • Specifying (tasks and information flows from which Procedures are derived),

  • Profiling (referenced events, organizations, roles and other entities), and

  • Improving (making changes to a plan to make it more manageable or more valuable)

Scoping a plan (these Activities initiate planning but can be iterated on throughout):

  • defining (incident) events and phases

  • listing participating organizations and members

  • adding a segment to the plan

  • listing organization goals per event phase

Specifying information sharing procedures:

  • specifying a task with its agents, needs, capabilities, effects...

  • connecting tasks with sharing flows defined in terms of info shared, channels, timing, impacts...

Profiling referenced entities:

  • profiling events, phases, organizations, roles, places, media, agents

  • as actual or types of entities (as applicable)

Improving a plan (reorganizing/restructuring a plan to increase its manageability or applicability, or making changes with global impacts on the generated Procedures):

  • moving tasks between segments

  • generalizing a task specification

  • changing an entity’s type tags

There is no fixed, prescribed order to the execution of Activities. Scope can be modified at any time while a Plan is being built. Tasks and information flows can also be specified at any time. Entities (events, roles, organizations, agents, phases, places, transmission media etc.) can be profiled as soon they are mentioned by name in the specifications of tasks and information flows, the profiles of other entities, or in the definition of the Plan’s scope. A Plan should be improved early and often in order to keep it as manageable and as valuable as possible.

Issues

The ISP Method defines a relatively large number of issues that relate to a Plan’s

  • validity (something is incorrectly specified or defined),

  • completeness (something is apparently missing), or

  • robustness (something makes the Plan failure-prone).

An issue can be about the Plan as a whole or about an element of the Plan (a task specification, role profile, segment definition etc.).

An issue has a severity of low, medium, high, very high or extreme. The severity of an issue is either constant or computed by determining the severity of a potential cascade of failures caused by the issue.

An issue comes with one or more alternate options for remediation. Some issues are “best guesses” and can be waived by Planners.

All defined issues can be automatically detected by software. Planners can report other, ad hoc issues; these will typically be domain-specific issues.

Moving to completion

When is a plan complete? Because one could always increase the scope of a Plan, a more useful question would be: Given its current scope, is there something clearly missing from a Plan? If the answer is no, then the Plan can be considered “complete”.

Nothing is clearly missing from a Plan if:

  • Each phase of each event in scope is addressed by at least one segment of the Plan (see definition of segment).

  • Each segment has at least one defined goal.

  • Each goal listed in a segment is achieved by at least one task within that segment.

  • All participating organizations and agents within scope are assigned tasks.

  • All information sharing flows imply sharing commitments by known agents.

  • All outstanding issues are waived.

The scope of a plan can be changed even after it is deemed complete. Plan tasks, flows and entities that do not contribute to completing a Plan are considered redundant. Redundant tasks, flows or entities should be removed from a Plan. If the scope of a Plan is reduced, some elements of a plan may become redundant.

There are three distinct strategies for driving a Plan to completion:

  • goals-driven,

  • effect-driven and

  • issue-driven.

Planners will usually use of all three strategies at once.

Goal-driven strategy

Planners look at each organizational goal attached to a phase of an anticipated event, specify tasks that achieve it, and the information needs of each of these tasks. They then specify tasks that produce the needed information and connect the tasks with sharing flows. This process continues until all goals are addressed by tasks and all task information needs are satisfied.

Tasks and flows thus specified and, consequently, the Procedures generated from them are necessarily within the scope of the Plan, since they all contribute directly or indirectly to addressing identified goals.

Effect-driven strategy

Planners start with the onset of an event phase, specify tasks that are directly triggered by it. They then specify the information produced by the tasks. and then the tasks that would be triggered or terminated by sharing this information. Planners connect the tasks with sharing flows as appropriate. This process continues until no task gets triggered or terminated by information produced by specified tasks.

Some tasks and flows specified using this strategy (and thus Procedures generated from them) might not all belong within the scope of the Plan since they do not necessarily contribute directly or indirectly to addressing identified goals.

Issue-driven strategy

Planners scan the list of detected issues. Issues can be about validity (incorrectly defined plan elements), completeness (plan elements obviously missing) and robustness (Procedures are resilient to failures). They then select an issue to be addressed and 1- waive the issue (if applicable and if the issue is a false negative), or 2- decide on applying a remediation Activity (possibly chosen from recommendations provided with the issue). The process is repeated until all outstanding issues are waived.

A Plan with no outstanding, “unwaived” issues could be considered valid, complete and robust, assuming that all possible material issues were indeed detected (an assumption that is impossible to verify).
