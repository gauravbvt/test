ISP concepts

Information sharing plan

An Information Sharing Plan specifies the information sharing commitments required of people and systems (agents) in roles of responsibility to fulfill the missions of their organizations, before, during and/or after events of significance.

Agents achieve goals that fulfill their organizations' missions by executing, individually or as teams, tasks assigned to them. By executing their assigned tasks, agents directly manage risks, achieve gains, and/or produce information needed by other tasks. Information sharing flows connect tasks that produce information with tasks that need it. Information can be shared between people, people and systems or between systems, over transmission media or face to face. Sharing can be subject to policies.

When planners define a task, they either identify or profile the agents to be assigned to it. Profiling task assignees can be done more or less broadly by specifying them in terms of their organizations, roles, qualifications and/or jurisdictions. Agents can be bound by information sharing commitments whenever they are assigned to tasks that are connected by flows to other tasks. Agents commit to share information they acquire when doing a task with some or all agents who execute flow-connected tasks. Recipient agents commit not to use the information for other purposes.

It is important understand that an information sharing plan is not an action plan. Unlike Business Process Modeling, the objective of ISP is not to optimize, script and automate activities. The overarching objective of building an IS plan is to ensure that information does flow as required in order to successfully manage risks and capture opportunities.

Task sequencing is however not entirely absent from an IS plan. A task triggered by information requires the prior execution of another task producing it. Such sequencing constraints do not typically apply to all tasks in an IS plan and thus an IS plan will most often not dictate a unique sequence for all tasks.

This is a good thing. Attempting to define a precisely orchestrated action plan for organizations without a central authority is almost always an exercise in futility. So, while an IS plan certainly captures what needs to be done, the focus is squarely on how information needs to flow to ensure that mission-accomplishing tasks are successful. The details of exactly how these tasks are to be executed is outside the scope of ISP.
