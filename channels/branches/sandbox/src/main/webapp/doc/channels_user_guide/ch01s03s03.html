Event phases, goals, and segments

The scope of an IS plan is primarily set by identifying the events of significance the plan must address. (A plan's scope is also set by the list of organizations that are expected to participate in the plan.) An event of significance (or event, for short) is something that is true somewhere for some time and that challenges, in and of itself, the mission of one or more organizations by introducing risks or by creating opportunities for gains. Organizations assign their agents to tasks that mitigate these risks or make gains. An event can bring different risks or opportunities to different organizations. For example, an explosion at a refinery, clearly an event of significance, endangers the lives of workers, would likely cause financial losses to insurers, and might profit commodity traders.

The events in an IS plan are not actual events that did or will occur at specific times and locations. They are implicitly types of events that might happen in some indeterminate future in some location that can be identified (e.g., in New York City) or specified (e.g., in a large urban area).

Figure 1.13. An event's definition


Risks and opportunities can exist in the before, during, or after phases of an event (event phases). Typically the purpose of tasks in a "before" phase of an event will be to cause or prevent the event. Tasks in a "during" phase of an event will normally be about responding to the event as it is unfolding in order to minimize its negative consequences or maximize positive outcomes. Tasks in an "after" phase will often deal with recovering from the aftermath of an event, or reaping its rewards.

An IS plan will typically focus on at least one, and at most a few, events of significance, and cover one or more phases for each of these events. Events within the scope of a plan are a combination of events that happen outside the control of the participants of the plan (so-called incidents) and of events caused by planned tasks (and are thus under the control of participants).

Figure 1.14. Events in the scope of an IS plan


An IS plan should also address only a small, manageable number of organizational risks or opportunities stemming from events in scope. It is recommended, when starting on a new IS plan, not to involve too many organizations having too many goals in too many phases of too many events. It is better to start small and focused and then grow as needed.

Information sharing plans typically deal with more than one event phase, and they can involve a sizable number of organizations. Because plans can become quite large, planners break them into smaller, more manageable units of planning called segments.

Figure 1.15. A segment defined


An IS plan segment covers only one phase of one event, possibly in the context of some other past or currents events. For example, a segment might focus on responding to a "stock price rally" (event phase focus) during "a volatile market" and after a prior "hostile takeover attempt" (context events).

A segment might address all or only some of the organizational goals associated with an event phase. In other words, more than one segment may be dedicated to a given event phase, with each segment addressing a different list of organizational goals. Two segments should not address the same goal for the same event phase.

Figure 1.16. Organizational goals addressed in a segment


A segment lists one or more organization goals (a segment without goals is an issue detected by Channels). A goal is either a risk to an organization (or type of organization) to be mitigated, or a gain to be realized by an organization (or type of organization). Each risk is categorized from a list of types of risks derived from actuarial risk management taxonomies. Each gain is also categorized from a list that is the "mirror image" of the risk types. Risks are rated as minor, major, severe, or extreme. Gains are rated as low, medium, high, or highest. Planners can indicate if a risk is implicitly mitigated or a gain implicitly realized once the event phase covered by the segment is terminated.

Every task and flow in an IS plan belongs to one and only one segment. A segment should contain all the flow-enabled tasks that directly achieve the segment's goals, plus flow-enabled tasks on which goal-achieving tasks depend to start, terminate, or succeed.

Figure 1.17. Goal-achieving tasks and supporting tasks


Segments are normally not disjoint; flows usually connect one or more tasks in one segment to tasks in another. Furthermore a task in one segment might initiate the event phase addressed by the other segment either by causing an event or terminating it. Initiation of an event starts the "during" phase of that event and terminates its "before" phase. Termination of an event starts the "after" phase of that event.
