Definitions

An Information Sharing Plan captures the information sharing procedures that people and systems in roles of responsibility are expected to apply in order to fulfill the missions of their organizations before, during and/or after certain types of events. A Plan is comprised of tasks and information flows. Tasks manage risks, achieve gains and/or contribute information to other tasks. Information flows connect tasks that produce information with tasks that require it.

An Information Sharing Plan can be abstracted from a specific context (a context is a collection of organizations with people and systems in their employ) so as to make the Plan generally applicable.

An event in ISP is something that happens or has happened somewhere and that introduces risks to organizations or to their missions.

A goal is either a risk or a gain. A goal is associated with the pre-, co- or post- event phase of an anticipated event. A risk is a possible, negative outcome and it is categorized according to an actuarial taxonomy of risks, and it is rated as more or less severe. A gain is the flip side of a risk. It is a possible positive outcome, and it is categorized by a taxonomy that mirrors the risk taxonomy. A gain is rated as either low, medium or high.

Information sharing plans will typically deal with more than one phase of more that one type of event. Because plans can become quite large, planners break them into smaller, more manageable units of planning called segments.

An information sharing segment covers one phase of one type of event, and it will typically focus on just a few goals of organizations. Segments usually depend on other segments. Two segments are co-dependent when information produced by a task in one segment is consumed by a task in the other, or when a task in one segment initiates the event phase covered by the other segment.

Segments are planning artifacts. They are not meaningful to non-planners. An information sharing playbook ignores segment boundaries when aggregating a person or system's task assignments and sharing commitments.

A task in ISP describes something done somewhere for some purpose by a person or system in a role of responsibility on behalf of an organization. The people or systems carrying out tasks are profiled by name, role, jurisdiction and/or organization. The executor of a task may be profiled only partially so that more than one person or system match the profile and get assigned to the task. According to a tasks' definition, those assigned to the task are expected to carry it out either as a team or on their own.

Tasks are considered useful if they:

  • achieve risk mitigation or gains,

  • initiate or terminate (phases of) events,

  • or produce information needed by other useful tasks.

Tasks can be triggered or terminated by:

  • awareness of events,

  • notifications they receive,

  • requests for information they receive.

Information flows link tasks that produce information to tasks that consume it. A flow is characterized by:

  • the information communicated (down to its elements, each with optional secrecy classifications and optional sourcing and handling instructions),

  • the mode of communication (request vs notification, unicast vs broadcast),

  • a list of preferred communication media (phone, face-to-face etc.),

  • the impacts, if any, the communication may have (it might trigger or terminate a task),

  • policies that mandate or prohibit sharing.

Task assignments are determined by matching the tasks specified in the Plan with the people and systems profiled in a context. People and systems are bound by information sharing commitments when they are assigned tasks that produce information consumed by other tasks.

The information sharing procedures captured by a Plan are the task assignments plus the information sharing commitments of all persons and systems in context.

An information sharing playbook is the sum of all information sharing procedures in a Plan for a given person or system.

Plans, segments, tasks, information needs, capabilities and flows are defined in terms of actual or types of agents, places, roles, organizations, events, communication media and phases of events. These are called entities. An entity in ISP is grab-all concept for something not planned that can be unambiguously referenced by name. Entities may themselves be defined in terms of other entities (an organization assigns roles to the persons it employs and systems it administers, etc.).

Actual entities (“Jane Smith”, “ACME Inc.”, “Morristown, Morris County, NJ, USA” etc.) are categorized by types of entities (“Senior executive”, “Corporation”, “City” etc.). Entity types can refine other entity types (a “Senior executive” is an “Executive”).

An agent is either a person or an information processing system (system for short). A system is capable of sending notifications, answering queries, receiving notifications or sending queries. An agent has contact information. An agent's clearance is a list of secrecy classifications. A secrecy classification has a name (“NATO”) and an ordered list of secrecy levels (“Restricted”, “Confidential”, “Secret”, “Focal Top Secret”, “Cosmic Top Secret”).

An organization is a group of agents organized to accomplish a mission. An organization assigns roles of responsibility to the persons and systems that act on its behalf. An organization has contact information and may contain other organizations. An organization may have information sharing agreements with other organizations to formalize the sharing commitments its members has with members of other organizations.

A role is a set of powers and responsibilities assigned by an organization to an agent acting on behalf of the organization. A role assignment may be restricted to a jurisdiction.

A place is either a geographical area (some city, county, state, country, natural preserve etc.) or a known location (some train station, building etc.). A place can be contained in another (for example, a building can be in a campus). An event and an event type can both be scoped to a place.

A communication medium is how agents transmit information. A medium is either unicast (transmits to a single, known agent), multicast (transmits to multiple, known agents) or broadcast (little to no control over who receives the transmission). Communication media may or may not be technology-based. “Telephone”, “Email”, “Instant Messaging” are examples of unicast communication media, and so is “Face-to-face”. “Notification system” and “Meeting” are both multicast media. “Television”, “Radio” and “PA System” are examples of broadcast media. A communication medium can delegate transmissions to other media (for example, VoIP can interface with Telephones). The security of a medium is expressed in terms of secrecy classifications.

ISP identifies more than 60 different kinds of issues about tasks, information flows, entities and entire segments and plans. There are issues of validity (definition errors), completeness (missing Plan elements) and robustness (something that makes the Plan failure-prone). An issue has a description, a list of suggested remediations, and a severity level.
