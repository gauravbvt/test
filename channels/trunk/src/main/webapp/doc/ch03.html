Overview

Channels encourages and supports a methodical approach to information sharing planning.

The main activities of this method are:

  • setting the scope of the Plan,

  • dividing the Plan into segments,

  • defining tasks and information flows,

  • defining referenced entities,

  • closing gaps in the Plan,

  • addressing other issues,

  • deploying the current version to participants.

The method here outlined does not address the politics and project logistics of information sharing planning.

When building a new Information Sharing Plan, planners must first decide on the scope of the Plan. The scope is set by choosing:

  • the anticipated types of events planned for,

  • the specific phases (before, during or after) to be covered for each event,

  • the organizations and roles which involvement is expected.

Planners divide the Plan into segments. Each segment covers one phase of one event and a small list of risks to organizations. Two segments can cover the same event phase, but not the same goal for the same phase of the same event.

A planner works on one segment at a time and can switch attention to another segment at any time. When working on a segment, tasks and information flows linking tasks are identified and defined so that they together address the risks covered by the segment.

Events, phases, agents, roles, organizations, places, and communication media are collectively called entities. Entities are referenced by name in the definitions of a plan's scope, a segment's coverage, tasks, information flows, and other entities. An entity can be referenced by name prior to being defined. An entity should be defined soon after having first been referenced.

The Plan is best grown by progressively closing gaps (completeness issues) such as those caused by:

  • events or goals not addressed,

  • unsatisfied task information needs,

  • information produced by tasks that is not shared,

  • tasks to which no known agents are assigned,

  • information flows with no listed communication media.

Planners can also be presented with issues other than completeness issues, namely, validity and robustness issues. Planners prioritize the resolution of issues possibly on the basis of their severity. Some types of issues can be waived if deemed “false positives”. When tackling an issue, planners may choose to first survey their peers and participants involved in the Plan about alternate remediation strategies.

The planning process is iterative and non-linear. At any point in time, planners can:

  • change the scope of a Plan,

  • add/modify/remove new Plan segments,

  • add/modify/remove tasks and flows in a segment,

  • resolve issues,

  • (re)define referenced entities.

At any point during the development of a Plan, planners may decide to deploy the current version to participants. If a previous version had already been deployed, it is immediately retired and superseded by the new one. Keep in mind that a Plan is never complete and planning is never done.

Deciding on the scope of the Plan

  • What anticipated events are being being planned for?

  • For each anticipated event:

    • What type of event is it?

    • At what place or type of place would the event happen?

    • What phase of the event is being planned for? Before, during or after the event?

  • What specific organizations are expected to be involved?

    • What are their names?

    • What are their respective missions?

  • What organizational roles are expected to be assigned tasks in the Plan?

  • What classification systems will be referenced in the Plan and what are their respective classification levels?

Dividing the Plan into segments

  • For each planned-for event phase, what goals must be achieved (risks addressed and gains made)?

  • For each risk:

    • Select the nature of the risk among:

      • property damage, property loss , injury, disablement or death , or environmental degradation (a material risk)

      • or cost increase, lost revenue, asset devaluation, reduced liquidity, reduced credit, increased liability, or fines or penalties (a financial risk)

      • or delayed delivery, impaired quality, process inefficiency, or supply chain disruption (an operational risk)

      • or opportunity loss, damage to reputation, loss of competitiveness, or product obsolescence (a strategic risk)

    • How severe is the risk (minor, major or extreme)?

    • What organizations or types of organization are at risk, in themselves or their missions?

    • Does the risk go away when the event phase is over?

  • For each goal:

    • Select the nature of the goal among:

      • property improvement, property acquisition, better health , or environmental improvement (a material gain)

      • or cost decrease, increased revenue, asset value increase, increased liquidity, increased credit, reduced liability, or reward (a financial gain)

      • or faster delivery, better quality, improved process efficiency, or improved supply chain (an operational gain)

      • or opportunity gain, improved reputation, greater competitiveness, or longer product life (a strategic gain)

    • How great is the gain (low, medium or high)?

    • What organization or type of organization seeks to achieve a gain, for itself or to further its mission?

    • Does the goal to achieve a gain go away when the event phase is over?

  • If the list of goals is long, how should it broken into cohesive sub-lists?

  • Create and name a segment for each (sub-)list of goals for an event phase in scope.

Defining tasks and flows in each segment

A task is either some activity or an act of decision making. A task is expected to either mitigate a risk, end or trigger a a phase of an event or produce information needed by another task. People and systems in scope are assigned to a task based on its specifications.

  • What tasks could terminate the segment's event phase? (Keep in mind that a task causing an event initiates the its co-event phase and terminates its pre-event phase, and that a task that terminates an event also initiates its post-event phase.)

  • What tasks would achieve one or more of the Plan's segment's goals?

  • What tasks start on their own with the event phase of a segment? (It is implied that assignees would not require notification of the event to become aware of it.)

  • For each task:

    • Name and describe each identified task.

    • Specify who would carry out a task:

      • Give the identity or type of an assigned agent, or not.

      • Give the organizational role of the explicitly or implicitly assigned agent(s), or not.

      • Give the jurisdiction for the role, or not.

      • Give the identity or type of organization of the agent, or not.

      • How long does it normally take to complete the task? Is the task self-repeating? How long does it take before it repeats?

    • Where is the task executed?

    • What goals does it achieve?

    • What are the information needs of a task?

    • For each information need of a task:

      • Name the information needed.

      • List the elements of information.

      • For each element of information:

        • Is the element classified?

        • Are there special instructions regarding the source or the handling of the element?

      • State whether the task's assignees expect to receive the needed information via notifications or as answers to requests.

      • List the communication media by which the needed information will be transmitted.

      • Give the time constraint for providing the information.

      • State what effect, if any, getting the information would have on the task:

        • Is it critical to its success?

        • Would it start the task?

        • Could it terminate the task?

      • What information does a task produce?

      • For each information produced:

        • Name the information.

        • List the elements of information and describe them as above.

        • State how quickly the information could be shared once available or requested.

        • State whether or not sharing the information would terminate the task.

  • Connect with information flows tasks that produce information with other tasks that need it and are allowed to use it.

  • For each information flow, identify any policy that either mandates it or forbids it.

Defining named entities

Entities are usually defined right after they are first referenced by name. In the definitions of tasks, flows, other entities etc. entities is referenced either as types (e.g. a Corporation) or as actual entities (e.g. ACME Inc.). An entity type can not be converted into an actual entity, and vice versa. Note that in ISP, there are no “actual” roles or events. Roles are always types and only types of events can be anticipated and planned for.

  • For each named actual entity or entity type to be defined:

    • Give a short description.

    • Name the entity types, if any, that categorize it.

  • For an agent (actual, not type):

    • Is the agent a person or an information system?

    • What are the agent's clearances, if any?

    • List the agent's contact information.

  • For an organization:

    • What is the organization's mission?

    • Does the organization have a parent organization?

    • Where is the organization located?

    • List the organization's contact information.

    • Who are the members of the organization that are expected to be involved in the Plan?

    • For each member:

      • What's the agent's title?

      • What roles does the agent play and within what jurisdictions, if any?

      • Is a formal sharing agreement required for each sharing commitment with another organization?

      • Is is acceptable for anonymous members of the organization to be assigned to tasks (e.g. “Some account manager at Nadir Insurance Inc. evaluates the claim”?)

  • For a place:

    • Is it within another?

    • Does it have a street address?

    • Can it be located geographically? If so, where?

  • For a type of place:

    • Are places of this type inside where a given planned event happens? Does a given planned event happen inside places of this type?

    • Are places of this type always inside some given place? Is a given place always inside places of this type?

  • For a type of communication medium:

    • Is the medium meant for unicast (transmission to one agent), multicast (transmission to many identified agents), or for broadcast (transmission to any and all agents)?

    • How are agents identified within this medium (e.g. via email addresses, phone numbers, IM handles etc.)?

    • Does this medium delegate to or interfaces with other media? Which ones?

    • How secure is the medium in terms of secrecy classifications?

Growing the Plan by closing gaps

A Plan can be grown by methodically systematically resolving completeness issues.

  • If no Plan segment addresses a phase of an event in scope, create such a segment.

  • If no goal is within the scope of a segment, add one.

  • If a risk is not mitigated or by any task and no task eliminates it (by terminating the event phase of the segment), then identify or introduce a task that does either. Or consider removing the risk from the scope of the segment.

  • If a stated gain with the scope of a segment is not achieved by any task, then identify or introduce a task that does so. Or consider removing the gain from the scope of the segment.

  • If an information need of a task is unsatisfied, identify or introduce a task that produces the needed information and would share it. (Then connect the tasks with a flow.)

  • If a task produces information that's never shared, identify or introduce a task that needs it and would consume it. Then connect with a flow. Or consider removing this capability from the task.

  • If a task is not useful (it produces no shared information and achieves no goal), see if it should achieve a goal, or could terminate the event phase, or share information it produces with a task that is useful. If not, consider removing the task.

  • If a task has no assignment (no known agent matches it), consider adding task-matching members to known organizations, or consider adding organizations to the plan with task-matching members.

Resolving validity and robustness issues

Validity issues are caused by incorrect definitions (they are bugs in the Plan). Robustness issues are detected weaknesses in a Plan that make it failure-prone.

Validity issues should be corrected as soon as they are detected.

  • What are the most pressing issues?

  • For a given issue:

    • Can the issue be waived? Should it be waived (false positive)?

    • What are the options for remediation?

    • Should other planners and selected participants be surveyed about remediation?

    • What's the appropriate remediation?

Deploying to participants

Work on a Plan is always done on its development version. There is always one and only one development version of a Plan. At some point the planners may agree that the development version should be deployed into production. Note that participants can only get their playbooks from the production version of a Plan.

When all planners vote in favor of deployment, a new production version is released, superseding any prior production version which is then retired. A copy is taken and made the new development version.

  • Is the development version of the Plan ready for release into production?

  • Is a new production release warranted?
