What is an Information Sharing Plan?

In a nutshell

An Information Sharing Plan captures the information sharing procedures (tasks and information sharing) that people and systems (agents) in roles of responsibility are expected to apply, within their jurisdictions, in order to fulfill the missions of their organizations before, during and/or after events of significance.

Tasks manage risks, achieve gains and/or produce information needed by other tasks. Information sharing flows connect tasks that produce information with tasks that consume it. Shared information is communicated over transmission media or face to face. Sharing can be subject to policies.

A task specifies, in more or less generic terms, which agents should be assigned to it, individually or as teams. Information sharing procedures are generated by matching agents within the scope of the Plan to the tasks. Agents are bound by information sharing commitments whenever they are assigned to tasks that are connected by flows to other tasks. These agents commit to share the information they produce with all agents who execute the connected-to tasks that consume it. An implied but important constraint is that information can only be used to execute tasks for which it is explicitly shared.

Plan segments

Information sharing plans typically deal with more than one phase of more that one anticipated event, and they can involve a large number of organizations. Because plans can become quite large, planners break them into smaller, more manageable units of planning called segments.

An information sharing segment covers only one phase of one event and addresses some of the related organizational goals. A segment will typically focus on just a few goals. Segments usually depend on other segments. Two segments are co-dependent when information produced by a task in one segment is consumed by a task in the other, or when a task in one segment initiates the event phase covered by the other segment.

Tasks and flows

A task describes an action carried out at some location and for some purpose by one or more agents. Agents act to fulfill the responsibilities of roles bestowed on them by organizations. The agents expected to carry out a task can be identified by any combination of name, role, jurisdiction and organization. The executor of a task may be identified only partially (for example, by role and organization only) so that more than one person or system will match the profile and get assigned to the task. According to a tasks' definition, those assigned to the task are expected to carry it out either as a team or on their own.

Tasks are considered useful if they:

  • directly achieve risk mitigation or gains,

  • initiate or terminate (phases of) events,

  • or produce information needed by other useful tasks.

Tasks can be triggered or terminated by:

  • awareness of events,

  • notifications they receive,

  • requests for information they receive,

  • the failure of a task.

Information flows link tasks that produce information to tasks that consume it. A flow is characterized by:

  • the information communicated (down to its elements, each with optional secrecy classifications and optional sourcing and handling instructions),

  • the intent of the flow (alarm, announcement, command, feedback or report)

  • whether it is executed only on task failure

  • the mode of communication (request vs notification, unicast vs multicast vs broadcast),

  • restrictions, if any, based on shared organization membership or location of the source and/or target of the flow,

  • a list of preferred transmission media (phone, face-to-face etc.),

  • the impacts, if any, the communication may have (it might trigger or terminate a task),

  • instructions on how to carryout the task,

  • policies that mandate or prohibit sharing.

Entities

Plans, segments, tasks, information needs, capabilities and sharing flows are defined in terms of agents, places, roles, organizations, events, transmission media and phases of events. These are called entities. An ISP entity can be unambiguously referenced by name in the specifications of tasks and flows, and in the profiles of other entities. An entity in ISP is anything that can be named while not something an agent does.

There are actual entities and types of entities. Actual entities (“Jane Smith”, “ACME Inc.”, “Morristown, Morris County, NJ, USA” etc.) are categorized by types of entities (“Senior executive”, “Corporation”, “City” etc.). Entity types can refine other entity types (a “Senior executive” is an “Executive”).

An agent is either a person or an information system (system for short). A system is capable of sending notifications, answering queries, receiving notifications or sending queries. An agent has contact information and availability (24/7 by default). An agent's clearance is a list of secrecy classifications. A secrecy classification has a name (“NATO”) and an ordered list of secrecy levels (“Restricted”, “Confidential”, “Secret”, “Focal Top Secret”, “Cosmic Top Secret”). An agent type can be thought of as a qualification that can be shared by different agents (e.g. "adult" or "registered driver"). An agent type is not to be confused with a role. A role implies responsibilities whereas an agent type denotes qualifications or status. An agent can be flagged as a system (non-human). When a group of agents are indistinguisable from a planning perspective (for example, call center operators), they can be profiled as a single archetype agent (for example, "a call center operator").

An organization is a group of agents organized to accomplish a mission. An organization assigns roles of responsibility to the persons and systems that act on its behalf. An organization has contact information and may contain other organizations. An organization may have information sharing agreements with other organizations to formalize the sharing commitments its members have with members of other organizations.

A role is a set of powers and responsibilities bestowed by an organization to an agent acting on behalf of the organization. A role assignment may be restricted to a jurisdiction (a place).

A place is either a geographical area (some city, county, state, country, natural preserve etc.) or a known location (some train station, building etc.). A place can be contained in another (for example, a building can be in a campus). An event and an event type can both be scoped to a place.

A phase names a time period relative to an event. A phase of an event is either set to before, during or after the event. Typical phases are “Preparing”, “Responding” and “Recovering”.

A transmission medium is a means by which agents transmit information to one another. A medium is either unicast (transmits to a single, known agent), multicast (transmits to multiple, known agents) or broadcast (little to no control over who receives the transmission). Transmission media may or may not be technology-based. “Telephone”, “Email”, “Instant Messaging” are examples of unicast communication media, and so is “Face-to-face”. “Notification system” and “Meeting” are both multicast media. “Television”, “Radio” and “PA System” are examples of broadcast media. A transmission medium can delegate transmissions to other media (for example, VoIP can interface with Telephones). The security of a medium is expressed in terms of secrecy classifications.

Issues

ISP identifies many different kinds of issues (80 and counting) about tasks, information flows, entities and entire segments and plans. There are issues of validity (definition errors), completeness (something obvioudly missing from the Plan) and robustness (something that makes the Plan failure-prone). An issue has a description, a list of suggested remediations, and a severity level (minor, major, severe, extreme). The severity is either a constant or it is calculated from the potential cascade of task failures attributable to the issue.

The meaning of information in ISP

Tasks and flows are defined in terms of information needed, uncovered and shared. The information is named and is broken into atomic elements. Each element of information is named, can be given security classifications and can be annotated with a description, handling instructions and with transformative relationships to other elements of information.

An element of information is considered atomic for the purposes of a plan; it is the indivisible unit of transmission and transformation.

The notion of the plurality of an element is left undefined. For example, if "suspicious activity report" information contains the element "suspect profile" then it is possible that there will be more than one suspect profiles in a report.

When analyzing flows, Channels is not concerned with what the information actually means. Rather it cares about whether or not two flows share the same information, and whether an element of information is the same as, or is the transformation of, another element of information.

If the information in two flows share the same name, does it mean that they represent, conceptually, the same information? Not necessarily. Two tasks performed respectively by a EOC commander and a corporate CEO and both needing a "situation assessment" would likely not need that same elements of information (even if the elements themselves have identical names).

In Channels, identically named flows, needs and capabilities (ignoring uppercase vs lowercase and extra spaces) are meant to represent the same kind of information (they are synonymous) only if they connect to the same task specification. Similarly, identically named elements of information represent the same atom of information only if they compose synonymous information.

Agents executing a task receive and send information. The information they receive and send may have different names an yet have common elements of information. For example, the "suspect profile" element of a sent "suspicious activity report" information may be the same as the "suspect description" element of a received "security tip", and it may be the same as the "suspect profile" of received "BOLO" information.

Alternately, an element sent may aggregate elements received. Aggregation combines many elements into a single element, with loss of data. For example, a "suspects count" element aggregates "suspect profile" elements into a single number, losing the details of the individual profiles. Counting is only one of many kinds of possible aggregations. A "threat level", for example, might aggregate a great deal of different elements into a rating.

In order to track the dissemination of information, it necessary to indicate when sent elements are renamed or are aggregated. Channels need only differentiate between two kinds of element transformation, renaming and aggregation, in order to track the dissemination of information. The description field of an element of information can be used to provide more details about the specific nature of an aggregation.

Issue detection takes element renaming and aggregation into account. For example, while it may be acceptable to declassify aggregated elements, it is not acceptable to declassify a renamed, classified element.
