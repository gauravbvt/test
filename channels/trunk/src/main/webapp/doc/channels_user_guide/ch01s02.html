Why Use Channels to Plan Information Sharing?

Planners use Channels, a Web application, to jointly build information sharing plans (IS plans). Planning information sharing is done ahead of anticipated events. Channels is not meant for managing the actual sharing of information during a crisis.

When building IS plans using Channels, planners clarify and uncover:

  • who needs what information, why, and when

  • who has or can produce needed information, how, and when

  • what information should flow, when, and how

  • flaws in information sharing practices

  • how people, systems, and organizations should be networked

  • the consequences of not sharing according to plan

  • where shared information originally comes from and ultimately ends up

Figure 1.1. Channels home page


Building an IS plan in Channels can be divided into

  • modeling information sharing practices (IS practices)

  • profiling the environment (organizations, people, and places) the IS practices apply to

When modeling IS practices, planners describe what kind of people and systems (or agents) are to be assigned to planned tasks. More importantly, planners describe what information would be acquired by the execution of a task and then shared with others who need it to carry out their own tasks. A model of IS would normally not identify task assignees by name, but rather specify them by their roles, qualifications, and/or jurisdictions and by the types of organizations they belong to.

When entering the profiles of people, systems, and organizations into an IS plan, planners do so with the expectation that profiles will match the descriptions of task assignees. Once assigned to a task, an agent can become committed to sharing information acquired by executing the task as specified by the (modeled) IS practices.

Figure 1.2. IS practices modeling interface


All modeling is done via input forms. At all times, even if only minimal data has been entered, an IS plan can be visualized, analyzed, diagnosed, and applied to people and systems profiled as members of organizations.

Figure 1.3. Sample IS modeling form in Channels - Information flow form


By using Channels to build IS plans, planners produce:

  • a reusable, transportable model of informations sharing (best) practices

  • actionable, personalized information sharing guidelines (IS practices applied to profiled agents)

  • information flow analytics and visualizations

  • detected information sharing gaps, bottlenecks, single points of failure, and other issues

A model of information sharing practices is visualized primarily as information flow maps. Information flow maps depict (specifications of) tasks connected by information flows.

Figure 1.4. Snippet of a flow map


Channels automatically detects issues in an IS plan (upwards of 90 different types of issues). Issues are communicated to planners visually (tasks and flows drawn in red) and in other ways as well (issues panels throughout, all-issues table, issues summary report). The entire set of outstanding issues is recalculated with every change planners make to the plan. Planners can manually indicate issues that Channels cannot detect, usually because the specific issues require domain knowledge.

An issue has a description, a list of suggested remediations, and a severity level (minor, major, severe, extreme). The severity of an issue is either a constant for the type of issue, or it is calculated from the potential cascade of task failures attributable to the issue, in which case the severity is set by the most severe risk not mitigated or the most valuable gain not realized because of the failure.

Figure 1.5. Examples of issue detection and reporting


Issue diagnostics is one of many kinds of analysis Channels performs on an IS plan. Another is failure impact analysis. Channels can show what would happen should a planned task not be performed or should some information not be shared as planned.

Figure 1.6. Failure impact analysis


Other questions Channels can answer about IS practices once modeled are:

  • Where would an element for information come from or end up according to plan?

  • Would it arrive in time?

  • How is it transformed along the way?

Channels applies the modeled IS practices to actual people and systems based on the roles they play in organizations. The results are personalized IS guidelines that can be accessed online by participants in the plan and printed.

Figure 1.8. Snippet from IS guidelines


By applying modeled IS practices to known people and systems in organizations, Channels can determine how organizations ought to be networked for an IS plan to be realized.

Figure 1.9. An organization's network based on an IS plan


While the effort required to model IS practices far exceeds that of simply drawing information flow maps using a drawing tool, the benefits of modeling IS practices using Channels are substantial and include the automated generation of personalized IS guidelines, issue detection, and analyses that illuminate the properties and consequences of applying these practices.

Channels is to information sharing what spreadsheets are to financial planning, and what Business Process Modeling is to business operations.
