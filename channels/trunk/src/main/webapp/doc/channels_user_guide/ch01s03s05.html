Entities

An IS plan references by name various agents, places, roles, organizations, events, transmission media and phases of events. These are referred to in ISP as entities. An ISP entity is anything that can be named in an IS plan but is neither a task nor information. An ISP entity is always unambiguously referenced by name in a plan, its segments, tasks and flows, or in the profiles of other entities. There can only be one agent named "John Doe", only one event named "lawsuit filed" etc.

There are actual entities and there are types of entities. Actual entities (“Jane Smith”, “ACME Inc.”, “Morristown, Morris County, NJ, USA” etc.) are categorized by types of entities (“Senior executive”, “Corporation”, “City” etc.). Entity types can refine other entity types (a “Senior executive” is a kind of “Executive”).

Actual entities can be categorized by entity types of the same kind (agents are categorized by agent types etc.) but entity types themselves can be categorized by other entity types, thus forming implicit typologies.

An agent is either a person or an information system (system for short). An agent is capable of sending notifications, answering queries, receiving notifications or sending queries. An agent has contact information and availability (24/7 by default). An agent's clearance is a list of secrecy classifications (e.g. NATO Top Secret and US Gov. Secret.)

An agent type can be thought of as a qualification that can be shared by different agents (e.g. "Adult" or "Registered driver"). An agent type is not to be confused with a role. A role implies responsibilities whereas an agent type denotes qualifications or status.

An agent can be flagged as a system (non-human).

When a group of agents are indistinguishable from a planning perspective (for example, call center operators), they can be profiled as a single archetype agent (for example, "a call center operator").

When the identity of an agent is to be resolved by associating the agent with one or more users of Channels, the agent is marked as a placeholder. (More on this later.) Agents are references in the definitions of tasks and of organizations (their members and their supervisors).

An organization is a group of agents organized to accomplish a mission. An organization assigns roles of responsibility to the persons and systems that act on its behalf. An organization has contact information and may contain other organizations. An organization may have information sharing agreements with other organizations to formalize the sharing commitments its members have with members of other organizations. Organizations are referenced in the definitions of a plan itself (organizations that are expected to participate), in segments (organizational risks), tasks and as parents of other organizations.

A role is a set of powers and responsibilities bestowed by an organization to an agent acting on behalf of the organization. A role assignment may be restricted to a jurisdiction (a place). A role is always an entity type. Roles are referenced in tasks and in organizations (roles played by their members).

A place is either a geographical area (some city, county, state, country, natural preserve etc.) or a known location (some train station, building etc.). A place can be contained in another (for example, a building can be in a campus). Places are referenced in a plan's definition (the plan's default locale), in events (an event and an event type can both be located to a place), in tasks (location of execution and jurisdiction of assignees), in organizations (address and jurisdictions of members).

A phase names a time period relative to events. A phase is either set to before, during or after events. Typical phases are “Preparing”, “Responding” and “Recovering”. A phase is always an entity type. Phases are referenced in a plan's definition (phases in scope), and in segments (event phase in focus and event phases in context).

A transmission medium is a means by which agents transmit information to one another. Transmission media may or may not be technology-based. A medium is either unicast (transmits to a single, known agent), multicast (transmits to multiple, known agents) or broadcast (little to no control over who receives the transmission). “Telephone”, “Email”, “Instant Messaging” are examples of unicast communication media, and so is “Face-to-face”. “Notification system” and “Meeting” are both multicast media. “Television”, “Radio” and “PA System” are examples of broadcast media.

A transmission medium can delegate transmissions to other media. For example, VoIP can interface with Telephones. The security of a medium is expressed in terms of secrecy classifications. A transmission medium is always an entity type. Transmission media are referenced in the definitions of communication channels. Communication channels are used to define information flows, needs and capabilities, and the contact information of agents and organizations.
