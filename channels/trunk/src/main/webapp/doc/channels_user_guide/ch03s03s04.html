Improving a Plan

A number of ISP Method activities do not change the scope of an IS plan nor do they add elements to it but, rather, are concerned with improving the plan overall. A plan is improved either by better distributing tasks and flows across segments, by generalizing a task's definition to increase the number of agents that are assignable to it, or by making changes to entity typologies that have wide-ranging effects on the generation of IS procedures.

The Channels-supported, plan-improving activities are:

  • Move tasks across segments

  • Generalize a task's definition

  • Change the categorization of an entity

Move tasks across segments

An IS plan is a collection of segments, each focusing on a specific situation, namely, one phase of one event, possibly in the context of other current or past events. Segments are usually inter-connected both by flows that connect two tasks in different segments, and by the fact that a task in one segment may trigger the situation that's the focus of another segment.

Each segment addresses one or more risks to or opportunities for organizations (goals) for a given situation. As more goals are added to the scope, more tasks are needed to achieve them and to provide needed information. A segment could grow to become quite large and difficult to manage (the flow maps become oversized, the list of completeness issues for the segment become hard to track etc.)

A solution is to create a new segment, move to the new segment tasks from the large segment that achieve or help achieve a goal, and then remove the goal from the initial segment.

Channels provides a simple and safe mechanism for moving selected tasks all at once to another segment while keeping them connected with their flows. Goals listed in moved tasks are added as needed to the destination segment. Moving tasks is a reversible (undo-able) change.

Table 3.19. Improving a plan: Move task across segments
Prerequisites:

  • The origin and destination segments are created

  • The tasks to move are created (of course)
Steps:

  • Select a segment of origin

  • Select tasks in the segment of origin to move

  • Select the destination segment

  • Remove goals from the segment of origin now addressed exclusively by the destination segment
Outcomes:

  • The segment of origin is of a more manageable size
