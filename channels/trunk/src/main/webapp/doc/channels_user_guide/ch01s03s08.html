Task assignments and information sharing commitments

A task in an IS plan is a specification of some action to be done or some decision to be made either all the time, upon witnessing the start or end of an event, or upon receiving a notification or a request for information. In defining a task, planners normally do not identify by name the actual agents to be assigned to the tasks, but rather describe their profile in terms of their qualifications (as agent types), roles, jurisdictions (as named places or types of places), and/or organizations (by name or by type).

What is meant by "directly or indirectly"? Let's say that FBI (an actual organization) is categorized as both a "Law Enforcement Agency" and "Federal Agency", and "Federal Agency" is categorized as a "Government Agency", then the FBI would directly match "Law Enforcement Agency" and indirectly match "Government Agency".

Any agent assigned to a task that sends information to another task (i.e. is connected by a flow targeting the other task) commits to share it with some or all agents assigned to the task receiving the information. A task assigned to three agents with a flow targeting another task assigned to two agents might imply six different information sharing commitments, one from each assignee of the information-producing task to each assignee of the information-consuming task.

If the flow is annotated as "to every", then the commitment is to share it with all assignees of the target task, otherwise the flow is "to any" and the commitment is to share with at least one of the assignees of the target task.

Not all assignees of the target task are necessarily eligible to be beneficiaries of information sharing commitments (commitments for short). Commitments can be subject to restrictions or prohibitions. Commitments can be restricted to agents in the same or different organization or in organizations with the same parent organizations. They can be restricted to agents in the same location or different location, to agents who are supervisors. Commitments can be restricted to one self, which may sound odd until one realizes that an agent might uncover information doing a task, keep it to oneself and start another task on the basis of this information (an agent has in essence notified itself to trigger another task). Commitments can also be restricted to "someone else" or to agents in both the same organization and location.

Figure 1.33. Sharing commitments derived from assignments to tasks connected by a flow


A flow can be marked as "prohibited", in which case no commitments are implied, no matter who is assigned to the source and target tasks. Flow prohibition is used to specify "exceptions to the rules" in IS plans. (More on that later.)

An agent's information sharing procedures (IS procedures) is the list of all task assignments for the agent plus all associated information sharing commitments. An agent's information sharing guidelines are produced from the IS procedures computed by Channels for that agent.

Figure 1.34. Computing information sharing procedures


Many analytics in Channels (dissemination, impact failure, network, various type of issues) depend on the computing of IS procedures for all agents in an IS plan. Because matching of profiles involves entity types, the proper design of typologies (for agents, roles, places, organizations etc.) is key to successfully building IS plans.
