How is ISP different from BPM?

The most frequently asked question about Information Sharing Planning is How does it differ from Business Process Management?

To answer this question, one must first consider that Information Sharing Planning (ISP) is part of something larger called Information Sharing Management.

Information Sharing Management (ISM) is a set of technologies and best practices that help communities of organizations share information more effectively in order to manage risks when preparing for, responding to, and recovering from actual events. Information Sharing Planning (ISP) produces the information sharing procedures that organizations apply when doing ISM. ISP analyzes these procedures for continuous improvement.

Business Process Management (BPM) helps enterprises become more efficient, more effective and more agile in how they operate. Business Process Modeling captures and analyzes their business processes for continuous improvement.

Because ISP relates most closely to Business Process Modeling, the question should be recast to How does Information Sharing Management differ from Business Process Management?

Other questions are Can ISM and BPM be used together?, and How do the markets for BPM and ISM compare?

Differences

BPM and ISM tackle different problems from different perspectives.

BPM focuses 'inward' on a single (but possibly extended) enterprise. It looks at how the enterprise responds internally to events, and at how it renders services to its customers. The end is maximizing value creation while minimizing costs. The means is business process improvement.

ISM focuses 'outward' on (often ad hoc) communities of organizations. It looks at how organizations depend on one another to get the information they need to execute tasks that achieve organizational goals. The end is minimizing the impacts of systemic risks and maximizing the capture of opportunities. The means is better information sharing procedures.

While there is conceptual overlap (both ISM and BPM involve tasks, events and communications) and even though they both use somewhat similar-looking visualizations (directed graphs), ISM and BPM have very different world views. BPM looks at the operations of an enterprise, whereas ISM looks at the information flows within a community of organizations. They also differ in their goals. BPM optimizes enterprise value creation whereas ISM reduces the impacts of systemic risks.

Similarities

ISM and BPM are structurally similar in a number of ways.

Both BPM and ISM are concerned with optimization. BPM optimizes the design and execution of business processes, whereas ISM optimizes information sharing procedures and their application.

BPM and ISM each has a modeling component (business process modeling, information sharing planning) and a deployment component (modeled business processed are deployed as work flows, information sharing plans are deployed as information sharing procedures).

Both BPM and ISM integrate with existing systems and technologies. BPM integrates with EAI (Enterprise Application Integration), SOA (Service-Oriented Architecture) and EBA (Event-Based Architecture) technologies. ISM integrates primarily with Communication Technologies, Enterprise Directories and SOA.

Finally, both BPM and ISM require the participation of analysts. BPM involves 'business process engineers' and ISM involves 'information sharing planners'.

Integration

Because BPM and ISM have such different world views, we should not expect either to eventually absorb the other.

There are however significant points of interaction between BPM and ISM. To name a few, business processes can be driven by events that become known through information sharing, and the business processes executed when carrying out risk-mitigating tasks can be optimized.

It is reasonable to assume that BPM and ISM products will, in some future, work together to their mutual benefit.

Markets

“The BPM market is one of the fastest growing software markets, estimated to grow tenfold in five years, from approximately US$500 million in 2006 to US$6 billion in 2011.” [3]

While BPM is a maturing field, ISM is still in its infancy. It is too early to attempt an estimate of how fast the market for ISM could grow or how big it could ultimately become.

However, ISM is applicable to all domains of joint human endeavor, from emergency response to business risk management. With the explosive growth in systemic risks, as underscored by the recent financial crisis, the value proposition of ISM in an increasingly inter-connected world is bound to become compelling.

As BPM products become consolidated among a small number of large vendors, ISM may represent a unique opportunity for small firms to enter and define a new market space. Established BPM vendors might also want to offer BPMintegrated ISM products in order to differentiate themselves from their competitors.
